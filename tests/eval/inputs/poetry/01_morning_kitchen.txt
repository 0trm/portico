Morning, Kitchen

The kettle's whistle is the same whistle
my grandmother heard, and her grandmother,
in a kitchen with a different window
looking out on a different street.

The water has changed. The metal has not.
The hand that lifts the kettle off the flame
has changed in every cell since yesterday
and is the same hand it has always been.

Steam rises. The cup waits where it always waits.
Tea leaves uncoil in the dark like ferns
under a turning sea. Something is being passed
hand to hand, mouth to mouth, fire to fire,

through every kitchen ever lit at dawn,
and we, half-awake, are the small priests of it,
not knowing what we tend, only that we tend,
only that the cup is warm, that the day begins.
