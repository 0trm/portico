The lighthouse keeper kept two ledgers. The first was the ledger the inspectors saw -- watch hours, lamp consumption, wind speeds twice a day in a script so even the entries from twenty years ago could pass for last week's. The second ledger was kept in the back of the boatshed, behind the cans of red-lead paint, and it recorded what the first one didn't: the names of ships that should have been there and weren't, and the names of ships there were no records of, anywhere, that nonetheless came past on certain nights.

His daughter had asked, once, why he kept it. He had said: "Someone has to remember the ones nobody believes were here." She had not understood, but she had nodded, the way a child nods when an adult has said something with too many syllables. Years later, after he died, she found both ledgers on the same evening. She read the first one for an hour and the second one until dawn. Then she lit a fire in the cold stove and burned the first ledger -- the official one. The second she carried home and put in the wall behind her own kitchen, where her own daughter, decades on, would find it and not know what to do with it either.
